What is Integration? The "Reverse" of Differentiation

                    

                        

                            In calculus, integration is one of the two fundamental operations, alongside differentiation. At its simplest, integration can be thought of as the reverse process of differentiation. If differentiation finds the rate of change (slope) of a function, integration finds the accumulated quantity. This leads to two primary types of integrals, both of which our integral calculator can solve.
                        

                        
    
                            
  • Indefinite Integrals: These find the "general antiderivative" of a function. If you have a function representing speed, its indefinite integral gives you a function for the distance traveled.
    • 
                            
  • Definite Integrals: These calculate the net accumulation between two points. Geometrically, this is famously interpreted as the "area under the curve." If you integrate a function for profit rate from January to March, you get the total profit over that period.
    • 
                        

                    

                


                

                    
Indefinite Integrals and the Constant of Integration, "+ C"

                    

                        
When you find an antiderivative, you're answering the question: "What function, when differentiated, gives me the function I started with?" For example, the integral of `2x` is `x²`. But `x² + 5` also has a derivative of `2x`, as does `x² - 100`. The derivative of any constant is zero.

                        
To account for all these possibilities, we add a constant of integration, `+ C`, to every indefinite integral. This represents the entire family of functions that could be the antiderivative. The most basic rule for integration is the Power Rule:

                        

                            ∫ xⁿ dx = (xⁿ⁺¹)/(n+1) + C
                        

                         
So, to integrate `x³`, you would get `(x⁴)/4 + C`. Our calculator not only provides this result but also shows the application of the rule in its step-by-step solution.

                    

                

                
                

                    
Definite Integrals: Calculating the Area Under a Curve

                    

                        
A definite integral, written as ∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx, gives a specific numerical value. This value represents the net signed area between the function's curve and the x-axis, from a lower bound `a` to an upper bound `b`.

                        
The method for solving this is given by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part 2:

                        
    
                            
  1. Find the antiderivative of f(x), let's call it F(x). (You can ignore the `+ C` for definite integrals).
    2. 
                            
  2. Evaluate the antiderivative at the upper bound: F(b).
    3. 
                            
  3. Evaluate the antiderivative at the lower bound: F(a).
    4. 
                            
  4. Subtract the two values: F(b) - F(a).
    5. 
                        

                        
Example: Calculate the integral of `x²` from 0 to 2.

                        
    
                            
  • The antiderivative of `x²` is `(x³)/3`.
    • 
                            
  • F(2) = (2³)/3 = 8/3.
    • 
                            
  • F(0) = (0³)/3 = 0.
    • 
                            
  • The result is 8/3 - 0 = 8/3.
    • 
                        

                        
Our calculator not only computes this value but also generates a graph of `y = x²` with the area from x=0 to x=2 shaded in, providing a powerful visual confirmation of what you've just calculated.

                    

                

                
                

                    
Key Integration Techniques Explained

                    

                        
Many integrals can't be solved with the simple power rule. More advanced techniques are needed, which our calculator can demonstrate.

                        
    
                           
  • U-Substitution: This is the reverse of the chain rule. It's used when you have a function and its derivative (or something close to it) within the same integral. By setting `u` equal to the "inner" function, you can simplify the integral into a much easier form. It's one of the most common and powerful techniques.
    • 
                           
  • Integration by Parts: This is the reverse of the product rule, used for integrating products of functions (like `x * sin(x)`). The formula is ∫u dv = uv - ∫v du. The key is to strategically choose which part of your function is `u` and which is `dv`.
    • 
                            
  • Trigonometric Substitution: This technique is used for integrals containing expressions like `sqrt(a² - x²)`. By substituting `x` with a trigonometric function (e.g., `x = a*sin(θ)`), the expression simplifies thanks to trigonometric identities, allowing it to be integrated.
    • 
                        

                    

                

                
                 

                    
Non-Elementary Integrals & Numerical Approximation

                    

                        
It's important to know that not every function has a "nice" antiderivative that can be written in terms of elementary functions (polynomials, trig functions, logs, etc.). A famous example is the integral of `e^(-x²)`, which is fundamental to statistics. For these, it's impossible to find a symbolic indefinite integral.

                        
However, we can still find the value of a definite integral for these functions using numerical approximation methods like the Trapezoidal Rule or Simpson's Rule. These methods work by slicing the area under the curve into a large number of simple shapes (trapezoids or parabolas) and summing their areas. While our tool focuses on symbolic integration, this is a key concept in applied mathematics and engineering for solving real-world problems.

                    

                

            

        

    


    
    

        

            

                
Frequently Asked Questions

                
Find quick answers to common questions about our integral calculator

            

            

                

                    

                        What is the "+ C" and why is it important?
                        
                    

                    

                        
The "+ C" is the constant of integration. It's added to every indefinite integral because the derivative of any constant is zero. This means there is an entire "family" of functions whose derivative is your original function. `x² + 5` and `x² - 100` both have the same derivative, `2x`. The `+ C` represents all possible constant values.

                    

                

                

                    

                        What's the difference between a definite and indefinite integral?
                        
                    

                    

                        
An indefinite integral finds the general antiderivative of a function, resulting in another function (e.g., ∫2x dx = x² + C). A definite integral calculates a specific numerical value representing the area under a curve between two points (e.g., ∫ from 0 to 2 of 2x dx = 4).

                    

                

                

                    

                        Why does the calculator show a graph for a definite integral?
                        
                    

                    

                        
The graph provides a geometric interpretation of your result. The definite integral represents the "net signed area" between the function's curve and the x-axis. By shading this area, the calculator helps you visualize exactly what the numerical result means, which is a powerful learning aid.

                    

                

                

                    

                        The calculator says my function can't be integrated. Why?
                        
                    

                    

                        
Some functions, like `sin(x^2)` or `e^(-x^2)`, do not have an antiderivative that can be expressed using elementary functions. These are called non-elementary integrals. While our symbolic calculator cannot find an indefinite integral for them, their definite integrals can still be calculated using numerical approximation methods.
